THE HISTORY

TIMELINE

1958: Tennis for Two is created by physicist William Higinbotham, an early interactive game displayed on an oscilloscope.

1962: Spacewar! becomes one of the first digital computer games, allowing two players to control spaceships.

1971: Computer Space, the first commercial arcade video game, is released.

1972: Pong, created by Atari, popularizes arcade gaming and marks the beginning of the video game industry.

1977: The release of the Atari 2600 home console brings gaming into households, popularizing cartridge-based games.

1980: Pac-Man is released, becoming a cultural icon and setting new standards for arcade games.

1985: Nintendo releases the Nintendo Entertainment System (NES), revitalizing the home gaming market after the crash of 1983.

1989: The Game Boy, a handheld console from Nintendo, is launched, paving the way for portable gaming.

1991: Sonic the Hedgehog is released by Sega, introducing a new speed-oriented gameplay style.

1994: The Sony PlayStation launches, marking Sony's entry into the console market and changing the landscape of gaming with CD-ROM technology.

1996: The Nintendo 64 introduces 3D graphics and popularizes four-player multiplayer gaming.

2001: Microsoft enters the console market with the Xbox, featuring online gaming capabilities through Xbox Live.

2004: World of Warcraft is released, setting the standard for massively multiplayer online role-playing games (MMORPGs).

2007: The Wii is launched, emphasizing motion controls and appealing to a broader audience.

2010: Mobile gaming begins to rise dramatically with the release of the Apple App Store, leading to the popularity of games like Angry Birds and Candy Crush.

2013: The PlayStation 4 and Xbox One launch, focusing on digital distribution and online services.

Indie games gain prominence, with titles like Minecraft and Undertale achieving critical and commercial success.

Cloud gaming emerges, allowing players to stream games directly to devices without high-end hardware.

Virtual Reality (VR) and Augmented Reality (AR) technologies are increasingly integrated into gaming experiences.

The industry continues to evolve with a focus on inclusivity, accessibility, and cross-platform play.
